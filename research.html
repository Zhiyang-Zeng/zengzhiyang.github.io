Ultrafast Control of Chirality

Chirality plays a central role in condensed matter physics, governing optical activity and symmetry-breaking phenomena in a wide range of materials. A central goal of our research is to dynamically control chirality using ultrafast light pulses, enabling access to nonequilibrium states that are not achievable under equilibrium conditions.

Using femtosecond pump–probe techniques, we investigate how selective excitation of lattice vibrations can transiently induce chiral order in otherwise achiral systems. These experiments reveal that light can couple to symmetry-breaking modes and drive coherent structural distortions on ultrafast timescales. Such control provides a powerful route toward manipulating optical and electronic responses dynamically, with potential implications for ultrafast switching and functional materials design.

Light-Induced Switching of Novel Ferroic Order

Ferroic orders, including ferroelectricity and ferroaxiality, are central to many functional materials. An exciting frontier is the ability to switch such orders using light on ultrafast timescales, enabling non-thermal pathways to control material properties.

Our work explores how tailored optical excitation can induce and reversibly switch ferroic order, particularly ferroaxial order, through nonlinear phonon interactions. By driving specific vibrational modes, we access transient states with altered symmetry and long-lived metastable configurations. These results demonstrate that light can serve as an ultrafast control knob for ferroic degrees of freedom, opening new possibilities for non-volatile optical switching and future ultrafast devices.

Induction of Chirality with Strain

Strain is a powerful tool for tuning material properties, offering a continuous and controllable way to modify lattice symmetry and electronic structure. Combining strain with ultrafast excitation provides new opportunities to engineer emergent phenomena.

In our research, we investigate how strain can be used to induce or enhance chirality in materials, either statically or dynamically. Using piezostack-based strain cells, we apply precisely controlled uniaxial strain to tailor symmetry conditions and explore new chiral phases. When combined with ultrafast optical techniques, this approach enables access to rich nonequilibrium behavior and offers a promising pathway toward controlling chirality and related functionalities in complex materials.
